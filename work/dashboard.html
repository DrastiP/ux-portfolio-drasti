IR Dashboard: Designing the Operational Infrastructure for a $9M Product Launch

How a centralized command center made Arctic Wolf’s new $9M IRR subscription viable at scale and earned recognition as an IDC category-defining product.

Role

Lead Product Designer

Timeline

3 months · Q1 2025

Team

Product · Engineering · IR

Recognition

IDC Market Note

01 — Context

A new product tier with no interface to support it

Arctic Wolf launched the Incident360 Retainer — a tiered IR subscription designed to shift the business toward scalable, self-serve delivery. The retainer exceeded 200% of sales projections, generating $9M ARR within 12 months.

But the product had a critical gap: there was no customer-facing interface. Without a dashboard, every entitlement question or readiness request would route to an already-stretched team, creating an operational bottleneck during rapid scaling.

The dashboard wasn't just a UI; it was the essential infrastructure that made the retainer viable at scale. We launched both simultaneously to prevent an operational nightmare.

02 — Customer Problem

Two failure modes: during a crisis, and before one

Before this dashboard, customers in a crisis were directed to a static web form. In high-stress events where systems are compromised, there was no clear "I'm under attack" action and no visibility into Arctic Wolf's next steps.

In the readiness phase, the failure was quieter but equally damaging. Customers had no visibility into their entitlements or completion of readiness activities, driving churn and wasted value.

My goal was to design for both modes in a single interface: decisive action during a crisis, and clear visibility in the preparation phase.
03 — Approach

Systems first, interface second

With no time for direct customer research, I front-loaded systems research by shadowing Command Center leads and interviewing IR managers. I synthesized these into a cross-product ecosystem map — a wireflow showing how dashboard actions triggered internal processes.

This early mapping prevented late-stage surprises and gave stakeholders a shared mental model. The systems research directly shaped design: for example, understanding how data was tracked told us exactly what structured information we could (and couldn't) surface to customers.

04 — Key Decisions

Balancing customer urgency with operational safety

Decision 01 — The breach button: maximum visibility, deliberate friction

The "Report a Breach" button dominates the top-right hierarchy to be unmissable during panic. However, since clicking triggers a costly response, I built friction into the next step: a confirmation screen and triage form. This ensures instant access while protecting the backend from accidental triggers.

Decision 02 — Protecting backend operations

Requesting readiness activities initiates intensive workflows. I designed a two-layer protection system: a confirmation modal and a 48-hour session block to prevent duplicate tickets. Clear system feedback closed the loop for the customer without requiring follow-up.

Decision 03 — Driving security behavior via completion psychology

I broke the IR Planner into visible categories (Response Team, Critical Systems, etc.), each tied to a visual progress bar. Transforming documentation into an actionable metric created natural urgency and significantly increased engagement compared to the previous experience.

05 — Strategic De-scoping

Killing the "Pizza Tracker"

I chose not to build a real-time incident status widget. First, data architecture relied on manual documentation by analysts mid-breach, making a live UI unreliable. Second, incident response is chaotic and variable, not linear like baking a pizza. A stalled progress bar would cause anxiety rather than confidence.

06 — Design System Contribution

Extending Fenrir, not hacking around it

Rather than building one-off UI, I collaborated with the Design Systems Lead to architect new components for the Fenrir system. This ensured consistency and gave other teams reusable patterns, even under a strict 3-month deadline.

07 — Impact

A category-defining operational success

The Incident360 Retainer exceeded 200% of sales projections and generated $9M ARR. The dashboard was the operational backbone that made this growth possible by enabling self-service and reducing manual handling by the IR team.

08 — What's Next

Iterating on entitlement engagement

Post-launch data showed customers weren't proactively using touchpoints. I designed a contextual notification system that triggers at 9, 6, and 3-month windows to nudge action without creating noise, ensuring customers extract maximum value from their term.

09 — Reflection

What I learned

Push for customer access early: Relying on internal proxies was valuable, but real user testing would have provided more confidence in how customers interpreted summary widgets.

Establish metrics before launch: Defining behavioral metrics upfront would have made capturing the right FullStory data easier from day one.

Optimize handoff: I’d explore tighter engineering collaboration to reduce implementation drift and time spent on late-cycle QA.
