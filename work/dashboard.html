Incident Response Customer Dashboard

Redesigning the breach experience — from a static web form to a centralized command center that meets customers at the worst moment of their professional lives.

Role

Lead Product Designer

Timeline

3 months · 2024

Team

Product · Engineering

Org

Arctic Wolf

01 — Context

Understanding incident response

Arctic Wolf's Incident Response (IR) service supports organizations during two very different moments: active security attacks — ransomware, data breaches, credential theft — and pre-incident readiness, where teams prepare response plans before anything goes wrong.

These two states demand radically different emotional and functional experiences from a product. One is a crisis. The other is calm planning. The same interface had to serve both.

MDR customers

Managed Detection & Response. Full Arctic Wolf subscribers. Already embedded in the platform — IR is one of several services they access. Familiar with the portal experience.

IR-only customers

Incident Response retainer only. Purchased IR as a standalone service. May encounter the portal for the first time during an active incident — the highest-stress moment possible.

02 — The problem

A form in the middle of a doomsday event

When a customer experienced an active security incident, this was their experience for requesting help from Arctic Wolf: A static web form and a phone number. No portal. No clear "report a breach" action. No way to manage readiness or update an IR plan.

In the middle of a crisis — when systems may be down, every second counts, and adrenaline is running high — the existing flow asked customers to slow down, manually enter information, and figure out the right next step on their own.

During high-stress security incidents, customers are forced to dig through outdated emergency response plans, leaving them overwhelmed and scattered at exactly the moment they need clarity.
03 — Research

Three lenses: stakeholders, customers, and the journey

Research spanned internal stakeholder interviews, customer interviews, and journey mapping. Each surfaced a different layer of the problem.

Stakeholder insight

Internal interviews with Arctic Wolf IR responders revealed a gap. When a customer called in a breach, the IR team had to manually gather context — company size, affected systems, IR plan details — before they could act. A structured digital intake would save critical time.

Customer insight

Several customers described the moment they realized they'd been breached as disorienting. They knew Arctic Wolf was their partner — but didn't know how to reach them fast. The web form felt like a dead end, not a lifeline.

"I knew we had a retainer with Arctic Wolf, but when the moment came, I didn't know what to click. I just wanted a big red button."

04 — Define

Framing the design challenge

How might we help customers stay oriented and take the right action during a security emergency — while also supporting readiness before an incident happens?

  • Initiate fast during an active breach: No ambiguity about what to do next.
  • Manage ongoing readiness: Tabletop exercises and readiness health tracking.
  • Live IR Plan: Editable, versioned, and surfaced at the right moments.
05 — Design decisions

Key decisions that shaped the dashboard

Decision 01 — Make "report a breach" unmissable

The solution: a persistent, prominent CTA with clear contextual framing — present always, but visually calibrated to the customer's current state.

Decision 02 — One surface, two modes

Rather than separate products, the dashboard transitions between modes. Breach mode is urgency-first; readiness mode is planning-oriented.

Decision 03 — Structured intake to accelerate handoff

The initiation flow guides customers through what information the IR team needs, reducing time-to-first-response and eliminating manual context-gathering.

06 — Outcomes

What shipped and what it achieved

A centralized, customer-facing dashboard within the Arctic Wolf Portal — enabling organizations to initiate emergency IR engagements, manage readiness, and update incident response plans.

Faster Incident initiation time
Clearer Team handoffs
100% Digital plan access
07 — Reflections

What I learned

The biggest shift in this project was recognizing that a customer mid-breach isn't in a state to navigate complexity. The design had to do the thinking for them — not just be usable, but be orienting.

Bridging readiness and incident response in a single surface required careful IA work. When two modes share a product, define the transition state explicitly.
